Chapter 3 · Problem 3.10

Original Question

The conductivity of 0.001 mol L⁻¹ acetic acid is 4.95 × 10⁻⁵ S cm⁻¹. Calculate its dissociation constant if Λm⁰ for acetic acid is 390.5 S cm² mol⁻¹.

Background Concept

Conductometric methods allow us to infer the extent of ionisation of weak acids from their ability to conduct electricity. The molar conductivity Λm measures the contribution of one mole of electrolyte to the total conductivity of the solution and is given by Λm = κ × (1000/C). For a weak monoprotic acid HA that partially dissociates into H⁺ and A⁻, the degree of dissociation α is the fraction of HA molecules that ionise. Because conductivity arises only from ions, Λm scales with the number and mobility of ions present; at a given temperature, mobilities are fixed, so Λm is proportional to α when referenced to the limiting (infinite dilution) value Λm⁰, where ions migrate independently. Thus, α = Λm/Λm⁰. Ostwald’s dilution law relates the acid dissociation constant to α and C: Ka = C α²/(1−α) for a monoprotic weak acid, valid at low ionic strength where activity coefficients are near unity. As the solution is diluted, α increases markedly for weak acids, so Λm rises steeply and tends toward Λm⁰. In practice, one measures κ, computes Λm, estimates α relative to a tabulated Λm⁰ (from limiting ionic conductivities of H⁺ and the conjugate base), and then evaluates Ka. Care must be taken with temperature control and contamination by CO₂, which can alter conductivity via carbonic acid formation. This approach provides an independent route to acidity constants that complements pH-based and spectrophotometric methods.

Λm = κ × (1000/C); α = Λm/Λm⁰; Ka ≈ C α² /(1−α)

Answer

Λm = 4.95×10⁻⁵ × (1000/0.001) = 49.5 S cm² mol⁻¹; α = 49.5/390.5 = 0.127. Ka ≈ 0.001 × 0.127² / (1−0.127) ≈ 1.85 × 10⁻⁵.

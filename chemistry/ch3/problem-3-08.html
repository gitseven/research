Chapter 3 · Problem 3.8

Original Question

The conductivity of 0.20 M solution of KCl at 298 K is 0.0248 S cm⁻¹. Calculate its molar conductivity.

Background Concept

Molar conductivity provides a concentration-normalised measure of a solution’s ability to conduct electricity and allows meaningful comparison between electrolytes and concentrations. It is defined as Λm = κ × (1000/C), where κ is the specific conductivity in S cm⁻¹ and C is the molar concentration in mol L⁻¹; the factor 1000 converts litres to cubic centimetres. Physically, κ depends on both the number of charge carriers (ionic concentration) and their mobilities (influenced by ion size, solvation, and solvent viscosity). For a strong 1:1 electrolyte like KCl, complete dissociation is assumed over a wide concentration range, but inter-ionic interactions (ionic atmosphere and electrophoretic effects) still reduce mobility at higher concentrations. As the solution is diluted, these interactions diminish, so Λm increases and approaches the limiting value Λm⁰ at infinite dilution, where ions migrate independently (Kohlrausch’s law). In contrast, κ may decrease with dilution because the number of charge carriers per unit volume falls more rapidly than mobility rises. Hence, Λm is the more appropriate metric when comparing different concentrations of the same electrolyte. Temperature control is crucial: higher temperature generally increases ion mobility (lower viscosity), raising both κ and Λm. In practice, to compute Λm one first calibrates the cell constant using a standard KCl solution of known conductivity, measures the sample’s resistance to obtain κ, and then applies the formula above with the sample concentration. The resulting units, S cm² mol⁻¹, reflect the conductance contribution per mole of electrolyte dispersed uniformly in solution.

Λm = κ × (1000 / C)

Answer

Λm = 0.0248 × (1000/0.20) = 0.0248 × 5000 = 124 S cm² mol⁻¹.

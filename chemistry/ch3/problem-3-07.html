Chapter 3 · Problem 3.7

Original Question

Define conductivity and molar conductivity for the solution of an electrolyte. Discuss their variation with concentration.

Background Concept

Electrical conduction in electrolyte solutions arises from the motion of ions under an applied electric field. The conductivity κ (S cm⁻¹) is the conductance of a solution slab of 1 cm length and 1 cm² cross-sectional area and depends on both the number density of charge carriers and their mobilities. To compare electrolytes independent of concentration and cell geometry, chemists use the molar conductivity Λm (S cm² mol⁻¹), defined as κ multiplied by the volume (in cm³) that contains one mole of electrolyte: Λm = κ·(1000/C), where C is the molar concentration (mol L⁻¹). For strong electrolytes (fully dissociated), Λm increases moderately with dilution because inter-ionic interactions (ion–ion attraction, electrophoretic and relaxation effects) diminish, allowing ions to move more freely. However, even at moderate concentrations, deviations from ideality persist and can be analysed with Kohlrausch’s law of independent migration at infinite dilution. For weak electrolytes, dilution increases both the mobility and the degree of dissociation (α), often causing a steep rise in Λm as C decreases; in the limit of infinite dilution, Λm approaches Λm⁰, the sum of limiting ionic conductivities. These trends underpin conductometric methods to determine dissociation constants (via Ostwald’s dilution law), equivalence points in titrations, and ion transport properties. Accurate measurements require calibration of the cell constant and correction for temperature, since ionic mobility and solvent viscosity are temperature-dependent.

Λm = κ · (1000 / C)

Strong electrolytes: Λm increases moderately upon dilution due to decreased inter-ionic interactions. Weak electrolytes: Λm increases sharply due to enhanced dissociation.

Answer

Conductivity is S cm⁻¹ of 1 cm pathlength, 1 cm² area. Molar conductivity Λm = κ × (1000/C). Λm rises with dilution; sharply for weak electrolytes, moderately for strong electrolytes.

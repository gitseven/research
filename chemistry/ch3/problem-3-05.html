Chapter 3 · Problem 5

Question

The molar conductivities at infinite dilution for NaCl, HCl, and CH₃COONa are 126.4, 426.2, and 91.0 S cm² mol⁻¹ respectively. Calculate the molar conductivity of CH₃COOH at infinite dilution.

Background Concept — Kohlrausch's Law

Kohlrausch's law states that the molar conductivity of an electrolyte at infinite dilution is the sum of the molar conductivities of its constituent ions. For CH₃COOH: Λ°(CH₃COOH) = λ°(CH₃COO⁻) + λ°(H⁺). We can find λ°(CH₃COO⁻) from CH₃COONa and λ°(H⁺) from HCl.

Visualization

Solution

From CH₃COONa: λ°(CH₃COO⁻) = Λ°(CH₃COONa) - λ°(Na⁺) = 91.0 - λ°(Na⁺). From HCl: λ°(H⁺) = Λ°(HCl) - λ°(Cl⁻) = 426.2 - λ°(Cl⁻). From NaCl: λ°(Na⁺) + λ°(Cl⁻) = 126.4. Solving: λ°(CH₃COO⁻) = 91.0 - (126.4 - λ°(Cl⁻)) = 91.0 - 126.4 + λ°(Cl⁻). λ°(H⁺) = 426.2 - λ°(Cl⁻). Λ°(CH₃COOH) = λ°(CH₃COO⁻) + λ°(H⁺) = (91.0 - 126.4 + λ°(Cl⁻)) + (426.2 - λ°(Cl⁻)) = 91.0 - 126.4 + 426.2 = 390.8 S cm² mol⁻¹.

Tips to Solve

  • Use Kohlrausch's law: Λ° = λ°(cation) + λ°(anion)
  • Find individual ion conductivities
  • Set up equations from given data
  • Solve for unknown conductivities
  • Check the final answer

Key Formulas

Λ° = λ°(cation) + λ°(anion); Kohlrausch's law for weak electrolytes

Chapter 3 · Problem 3.4

Original Question

Calculate the standard cell potentials of galvanic cell in which the following reactions take place: (i) 2Cr(s) + 3Cd²⁺(aq) → 2Cr³⁺(aq) + 3Cd (ii) Fe²⁺(aq) + Ag⁺(aq) → Fe³⁺(aq) + Ag(s). Calculate the ΔᵣG° and equilibrium constants.

Background Concept — E°, ΔG°, and K

The thermodynamics of an electrochemical reaction at standard conditions links electrical potential, free energy, and the equilibrium position. The standard cell potential E° quantifies the driving force for electron transfer when reactants and products are all at unit activity. It is found by pairing half-reactions as reductions and subtracting the anode potential: E° = E°cathode − E°anode. The Gibbs free energy change ΔG° relates to E° through ΔG° = −n F E°, where n is the total number of electrons transferred and F is the Faraday constant (96485 C mol⁻¹). A positive E° gives a negative ΔG°, indicating spontaneity. The equilibrium constant K is connected to ΔG° via ΔG° = −R T ln K, so combining gives ln K = n F E° / (R T). Thus, even a modest positive E° can correspond to a very large K because of the factor nF/RT (~39 at 298 K per volt). In practice, one identifies the relevant half-reactions from tabulated standard potentials, ensures electrons are balanced (multiplying half-reactions as needed without altering their E° values), and then computes E°, ΔG°, and K. These relationships are invaluable for predicting reaction feasibility, estimating product distributions at equilibrium, and comparing alternative oxidant–reductant pairs in battery or corrosion applications.

Answer

(i) 2Cr + 3Cd²⁺ → 2Cr³⁺ + 3Cd

Using typical E°: Cd²⁺/Cd = −0.403 V; Cr³⁺/Cr = −0.740 V.

E° = (−0.403) − (−0.740) = +0.337 V; n = 6. ΔG° = −6×96485×0.337 ≈ −1.95×10⁵ J mol⁻¹ (−195 kJ mol⁻¹). K ≈ exp(6×96485×0.337/(8.314×298)) ≈ 1×10¹¹.

(ii) Fe²⁺ + Ag⁺ → Fe³⁺ + Ag

E° = 0.800 − 0.771 = +0.029 V; n = 1. ΔG° ≈ −2.8 kJ mol⁻¹. K ≈ 3.2.

Tips to Solve

  • Pick cathode/anode from tabulated E° values; do not change E° when multiplying half-reactions.
  • Balance electrons to find n; then use ΔG° = −nFE° and ln K = nFE°/RT.
  • Remember unit conversions: J vs kJ, and use T = 298 K unless otherwise specified.
  • Small positive E° can still imply large K because nF/RT ≈ 39 V⁻¹ at 298 K.

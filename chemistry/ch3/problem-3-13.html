Electrochemistry · Problem 3.13

Original Question

The amount of electricity (in terms of Faraday) required to produce: (i) 20.0 g of Ca from molten CaCl₂ (ii) 40.0 g of Al from molten Al₂O₃

Background Concept — Faraday’s Law and Metal Ion Charge

Faraday’s first law states that the mass (m) of a substance produced at an electrode is proportional to the total charge (Q) passed: m = (M z⁻¹) (Q/F), where M is molar mass, z is the number of electrons per ion, and F is Faraday’s constant (96485 C mol⁻¹). Expressed in Faradays, the charge in moles of electrons equals z·(m/M). For calcium and aluminium, the reduction steps are Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Ca and Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al. Thus z = 2 for Ca and z = 3 for Al. Compute moles of metal from mass and molar mass (Ca ≈ 40.08 g mol⁻¹; Al ≈ 26.98 g mol⁻¹), then multiply by z to obtain moles of electrons, i.e., Faradays. Industrially, current efficiency and side reactions can change actual charge required; however, stoichiometric Faraday counts provide the theoretical minimum. This approach generalizes to electroplating and electrolytic production where the valence state dictates electron requirement per atom formed. Care must be taken with hydrated salts versus molten electrolytes; here, molten salts are specified, so water electrolysis is not a complicating factor.

Answer

(i) Ca: n(Ca) = 20.0/40.08 ≈ 0.499 mol; electrons = 2×0.499 ≈ 0.998 F ≈ 1.00 F
(ii) Al: n(Al) = 40.0/26.98 ≈ 1.482 mol; electrons = 3×1.482 ≈ 4.446 F ≈ 4.45 F

So, ≈ 1.00 F for Ca and ≈ 4.45 F for Al (theoretical).

Tips to Solve

  • Use electrons (mol) = z × (mass/molar mass).
  • 1 Faraday corresponds to 1 mol of electrons.

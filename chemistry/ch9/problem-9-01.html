Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.1

Original Question

Explain the bonding in coordination compounds in terms of Werner's postulates.

Background Concept — Werner’s Coordination Theory

Werner’s postulates (1893) laid the structural foundation for coordination chemistry. He distinguished between primary valency (now oxidation state) and secondary valency (coordination number). The primary valency is satisfied by anions and is ionisable; the secondary valency is satisfied by ligands (neutral or anionic) directly bonded to the central metal in fixed directions, yielding definite coordination numbers and geometries (e.g., 6 → octahedral, 4 → tetrahedral/square planar). Complexes maintain their identity in solution, explaining why some ions are not precipitated by typical reagents: inner-sphere ligands are non‑ionisable and not displaced easily. Werner rationalised isomerism (geometrical, optical) by spatial arrangement of ligands around the metal. Though pre‑electronic, the theory predicted accurate structures confirmed later by X‑ray crystallography. Modern views (VBT, CFT, and molecular orbital theory) refine the description: metal–ligand bonds involve donor–acceptor interactions where ligands donate electron pairs into vacant metal orbitals; crystal field splitting explains colors and magnetism; and covalency/back‑bonding account for trends in stability and reactivity.

Answer

  • Metals exhibit a fixed coordination number (secondary valency) with directional bonding to ligands.
  • Primary valency equals oxidation state; satisfied by ionisable counter‑ions.
  • Complexes retain identity in solution; inner-sphere ligands are non‑ionisable.
  • Spatial arrangement of ligands explains isomerism (cis/trans, fac/mer, optical).
Example: [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃ → 6 secondary valencies to NH₃ (non‑ionisable), 3 primary to Cl⁻ (ionisable)

Tips to Solve

  • State primary vs secondary valency with one worked example.
  • Mention geometry and isomerism as key predictions.
  • Relate briefly to modern bonding models for context.

Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.4

Original Question

What is meant by unidentate, didentate and ambidentate ligands? Give two examples for each.

Background Concept — Denticity and Ambidentate Binding

Denticity refers to the number of donor atoms of a ligand that simultaneously bind to a metal center. Unidentate ligands bind through a single donor atom (e.g., NH₃, H₂O, Cl⁻). Didentate ligands use two donor atoms to form a chelate ring with the metal (e.g., ethane‑1,2‑diamine “en”, oxalate C₂O₄²⁻). Ambidentate ligands possess two (or more) potential donor atoms but bind through only one at a time; classic examples include nitrito (NO₂⁻) which can bind via N (nitro) or O (nitrito‑O), and thiocyanato (SCN⁻) which can bind via S (thiocyanato‑S) or N (isothiocyanato‑N). Ambidentate binding leads to linkage isomerism when the point of attachment changes.

Answer

  • Unidentate: NH₃ (ammine), H₂O (aqua); Cl⁻ (chlorido), CN⁻ (cyanido)
  • Didentate: Ethane‑1,2‑diamine (en), Oxalate (C₂O₄²⁻)
  • Ambidentate: NO₂⁻ (nitro vs nitrito‑O), SCN⁻ (thiocyanato‑S vs isothiocyanato‑N)

Tips to Solve

  • Define each term concisely and list exactly two examples.
  • For ambidentate, mention the alternative donor atoms and linkage isomerism.

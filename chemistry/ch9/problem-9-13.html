Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.13

Original Question

Aqueous copper sulphate solution (blue) gives: (i) a green precipitate with aqueous potassium fluoride and (ii) a bright green solution with aqueous potassium chloride. Explain these experimental results.

Background Concept — Ligand Exchange, Geometry, and Color

Aqueous CuSO₄ contains predominantly hexaaquacopper(II), [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺, which is blue due to d–d transitions in a distorted octahedral field. Addition of halides or fluoride can induce ligand substitution and formation of halido complexes with different geometries and ligand field strengths, altering observed colors and solubilities. Fluoride (a hard, small ligand) tends to form sparingly soluble mixed aqua/fluoro complexes or basic salts that can precipitate under certain conditions, often giving greenish solids due to altered crystal field environments. Chloride commonly forms tetrahedral [CuCl₄]²⁻ in concentrated chloride media; tetrahedral fields are weaker (smaller Δ) and shift absorption bands, often yielding yellow‑green to bright green solutions. Thus, green precipitate with KF and bright green solution with KCl arise from distinct coordination environments around Cu²⁺: insoluble fluoro species vs soluble chloro complexes with changed geometry.

Answer

  • (i) With KF: Formation of sparingly soluble fluoro/aquacupric species (e.g., CuF₂·xH₂O or mixed [CuFₓ(H₂O)₆₋ₓ]) gives a green precipitate due to modified ligand field.
  • (ii) With KCl (excess): Chloride substitution forms tetrahedral [CuCl₄]²⁻ (or mixed chloro‑aqua species), giving a bright green solution.
Cu²⁺(aq) + 4Cl⁻ ⇌ [CuCl₄]²⁻ (tetrahedral, green)

Tips to Solve

  • Invoke ligand substitution and geometry change (octahedral → tetrahedral).
  • Relate color changes to crystal field splitting differences.
  • Note precipitation vs solution based on complex solubility.

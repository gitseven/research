Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.30

Original Question

The most stable complex is: (i) [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (ii) [Fe(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (iii) [Fe(C₂O₄)₃]³⁻ (iv) [FeCl₆]³⁻

Background Concept — Chelate Effect and Ligand Field

Complex stability trends reflect ligand denticity (chelate effect), ligand field strength, and charge distribution. Bidentate ligands like oxalate (C₂O₄²⁻) produce chelate rings and significantly enhance thermodynamic stability compared with monodentate ligands. Among the options, [Fe(C₂O₄)₃]³⁻ is a tris‑chelate complex (three bidentate oxalates, CN = 6), benefiting from strong chelation entropy and often favorable field strength. Hexaammine and hexaaqua Fe(III) are less stabilized (monodentate ligands), and hexachloridoferrate(III) with weak‑field halides is also less stable. Therefore, the tris‑oxalato complex is expected to be the most stable.

Answer

Most stable: (iii) [Fe(C₂O₄)₃]³⁻ (tris‑chelate; strong chelate effect)

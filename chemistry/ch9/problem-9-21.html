Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.21

Original Question

Explain why [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ and [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺ are of different colours in dilute solutions.

Background Concept — Ligand Field, Metal Oxidation, and Charge-Transfer

Observed color originates from electronic transitions. In transition-metal complexes, primary contributions come from d–d transitions within split d-levels and, in strongly covalent systems, from ligand-to-metal or metal-to-ligand charge-transfer (LMCT/MLCT). The complexes [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ (ferrocyanide) and [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺ differ in both ligand field strength and electronic structure. In ferrocyanide, Fe is +2 in a strong-field, π-acceptor CN⁻ environment that significantly increases the crystal field splitting Δ₀ and enhances covalency and potential charge-transfer bands. This usually yields intense colors and shifts absorption to different wavelengths than aqua complexes. In contrast, [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺ contains Fe²⁺ with weak-field H₂O ligands, producing a smaller Δ₀ and typically weaker, Laporte-forbidden d–d bands partially allowed via vibronic coupling, hence paler or differently tinted colors. Furthermore, CN⁻, being a π-acceptor, stabilizes low-spin configurations, modifying the t₂g–e_g energy gap and selection rules relative to high-spin aqua species. Therefore, differences in Δ₀ magnitude, spin state (often low-spin in cyanide vs high/variable with water), and the presence of intense charge-transfer transitions in cyanide complexes account for distinct colors in dilute solutions.

Answer

  • [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ (Fe²⁺, strong-field CN⁻): large Δ₀, low-spin, significant LMCT → distinct, often more intense color.
  • [Fe(H₂O)₆]²⁺ (Fe²⁺, weak-field H₂O): smaller Δ₀, high-spin, weak d–d bands → different (usually paler) color.

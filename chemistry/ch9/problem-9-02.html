Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.2

Original Question

FeSO₄ solution mixed with (NH₄)₂SO₄ solution in 1:1 molar ratio gives the test of Fe²⁺ ion but CuSO₄ solution mixed with aqueous ammonia in 1:4 molar ratio does not give the test of Cu²⁺ ion. Explain why?

Background Concept — Complexation and Masking in Qualitative Analysis

Qualitative tests detect “free” metal ions capable of forming characteristic precipitates or colors with reagents. If a metal ion is strongly complexed by a ligand, its effective free concentration drops, and classical tests may fail (“masked”). In Mohr’s salt, FeSO₄ + (NH₄)₂SO₄ yields FeSO₄·(NH₄)₂SO₄·6H₂O; sulfate and ammonium ions do not form strong inner‑sphere complexes with Fe²⁺ in water, so Fe²⁺ remains available to respond to Fe²⁺ tests (e.g., Turnbull’s blue). By contrast, Cu²⁺ with excess NH₃ forms the deep blue tetraammine complex [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ with large formation constant (β₄), drastically reducing free Cu²⁺. Consequently, standard Cu²⁺ tests (e.g., with sulfide at low [S²⁻], or simple color tests) may be suppressed unless conditions are adjusted to break or outcompete the complex. Complex stability depends on ligand field stabilization, entropy of ligand binding, and hard–soft interactions; NH₃ is a good σ‑donor stabilizing Cu²⁺, whereas SO₄²⁻/NH₄⁺ are primarily outer‑sphere in these media.

Answer

  • FeSO₄ + (NH₄)₂SO₄: No strong inner‑sphere complex; Fe²⁺ remains detectable → Fe²⁺ tests positive.
  • CuSO₄ + 4NH₃: Forms [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ (deep blue), substantially lowering free Cu²⁺ → Cu²⁺ tests suppressed.
Cu²⁺ + 4NH₃ ⇌ [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺ (β₄ ≫ 1, strong complex)

Tips to Solve

  • State: strong complex formation “masks” the ion from tests.
  • Contrast weak outer‑sphere association (Fe²⁺/SO₄²⁻) vs strong inner‑sphere complex ([Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺).
  • Mention deep blue color as evidence of complex formation.

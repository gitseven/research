Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.20

Original Question

Explain why a solution of [Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is green but a solution of [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ is colourless.

Background Concept — d–d Transitions and Selection Rules

Colors of transition‑metal complexes arise mainly from d–d transitions between split d levels. The energy and intensity depend on ligand field strength (Δ), geometry, and selection rules (Laporte and spin). [Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is octahedral with weak‑field H₂O, producing a moderate Δ₀ and allowing spin‑allowed, vibronically relaxed d–d transitions in the visible region, giving a green color. [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ is square‑planar with strong‑field CN⁻, which produces large splitting and a low‑lying, fully paired d⁸ configuration. The relevant d–d transitions may be shifted to higher energy (UV) and/or Laporte/spin forbidden, resulting in very weak absorption in the visible and hence an apparently colourless solution. Additionally, strong π‑acceptor behavior of CN⁻ enhances Δ and reduces visible‑range absorptions.

Answer

  • [Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺ (octahedral, weak field): visible d–d absorptions → green solution.
  • [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ (square planar, strong field): d–d bands shifted to UV/very weak → colourless.

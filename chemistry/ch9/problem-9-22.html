Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.22

Original Question

Discuss the nature of bonding in metal carbonyls.

Background Concept — Synergic σ Donation and π Back‑bonding

Metal carbonyls (e.g., Ni(CO)₄, Fe(CO)₅, Cr(CO)₆) are archetypal organometallics stabilized by a synergic bonding mechanism between the metal center and the carbon monoxide ligand. In the σ component, the filled lone pair on CO (largely on carbon) donates electron density into an empty metal orbital (commonly of s/p/d character), forming a metal–carbon σ bond. Complementarily, in the π component, filled metal d orbitals of appropriate symmetry donate electron density back into the empty CO π* antibonding orbitals. This π back‑donation strengthens the metal–CO interaction and weakens the internal C≡O bond (lowering the C–O stretching frequency in IR relative to free CO at ≈2143 cm⁻¹). The extent of back‑bonding increases with metal electron richness (low oxidation state, high d‑electron count) and decreases as the ligand’s π* orbitals become more populated. As a result, carbonyl complexes favor low formal oxidation states (often zero) and obey the 18‑electron rule for stability, with coordination numbers and geometries (tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, octahedral) dictated by electron count and sterics. Substituting CO with π‑acceptor ligands (e.g., phosphites) or π‑donors (e.g., halides) tunes the back‑bonding and thus reactivity, enabling ligand substitution, migratory insertion, and oxidative addition—key steps in homogeneous catalysis.

Answer

  • σ donation: CO lone pair donates to an empty metal orbital (M←:CO).
  • π back‑bonding: filled metal d orbitals donate into CO π* (M→CO).
  • Back‑bonding weakens C–O (νCO decreases) and strengthens M–C.
  • Low metal oxidation states and 18‑electron configurations are favored.
IR diagnostic: νCO (complex) < 2143 cm⁻¹ (free CO) when back‑bonding is significant.

Tips to Solve

  • Mention σ donation + π back‑bonding synergy explicitly.
  • Relate νCO shifts to back‑bonding strength.
  • Note preference for low oxidation state metals and 18e rule.

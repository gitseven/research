Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.3

Original Question

Explain with two examples each of the following terms: coordination entity, ligand, coordination number, coordination polyhedron, homoleptic and heteroleptic.

Background Concept — Vocabulary of Coordination Chemistry

Coordination chemistry uses a precise vocabulary to describe the composition and geometry of complexes. A coordination entity consists of a central metal atom/ion bound to surrounding ligands, which donate electron pairs to form coordinate bonds. The number of donor atoms directly attached to the metal is the coordination number, often 2, 4, or 6 in common complexes, leading to characteristic polyhedra such as linear, tetrahedral, square planar, or octahedral. A homoleptic complex contains only one type of ligand; a heteroleptic complex contains two or more types. Ligands can be neutral (H₂O, NH₃, CO) or anionic (Cl⁻, CN⁻); some are multidentate (en, oxalate) and chelate the metal, increasing complex stability via the chelate effect. Recognizing these terms allows quick parsing of formulas, prediction of possible isomers, and understanding of reactivity and properties.

Answer

  • Coordination entity: [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺, [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻
  • Ligand: NH₃ (ammine), H₂O (aqua); Cl⁻ (chlorido), CN⁻ (cyanido)
  • Coordination number: 6 in [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺; 4 in [NiCl₄]²⁻
  • Coordination polyhedron: Octahedral [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺; square planar [PtCl₂(NH₃)₂]
  • Homoleptic: [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺; [Ni(CO)₄]
  • Heteroleptic: [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺; [Cr(NH₃)₅Cl]²⁺

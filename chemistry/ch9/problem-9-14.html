Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.14

Original Question

What is the coordination entity formed when excess of aqueous KCN is added to an aqueous solution of copper sulphate? Why is it that no precipitate of copper sulphide is obtained when H₂S(g) is passed through this solution?

Background Concept — Strong Complexation Masks Qualitative Tests

Cyanide is a strong field ligand that forms very stable complexes with many transition metals. With Cu²⁺ in excess CN⁻, stepwise complexation proceeds to form tetracyanidocuprate(II), [Cu(CN)₄]²⁻, or in some conditions, mixed Cu(I) species due to redox disproportionation in the presence of cyanide. The overall result is a drastic decrease in free Cu²⁺ concentration. Classical qualitative tests such as precipitation of CuS by H₂S rely on sufficient free Cu²⁺ to form the insoluble sulfide. When copper is sequestered in a highly stable complex, the solubility product of CuS is not exceeded, and precipitation is suppressed (“masking”). This principle underlies separation schemes in qualitative analysis, where complexing agents selectively keep certain metal ions in solution.

Answer

Excess CN⁻ + Cu²⁺ → [Cu(CN)₄]²⁻ (very stable); free [Cu²⁺] ≪ 1

No CuS precipitate forms upon passing H₂S because copper remains complexed; the ionic product for CuS does not exceed Ksp.

Tips to Solve

  • Name the complex: tetracyanidocuprate(II).
  • State the masking effect: free Cu²⁺ too low for CuS precipitation.
  • Mention strong field/large stability constant of CN⁻ complexes.

Coordination Compounds · Problem 9.25

Original Question

Explain the violet colour of the complex [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺ on the basis of crystal field theory.

Background Concept — d¹ Octahedral CFT and d–d Transitions

In [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺, titanium is in +3 oxidation state with electron configuration d¹. In an octahedral ligand field created by six water molecules, the five d orbitals split into a lower‑energy t₂g triplet and a higher‑energy e_g doublet, separated by crystal field splitting Δ₀. With a single d electron, the ground state is t₂g¹. When visible light passes through the solution, photons with energy close to Δ₀ can be absorbed to promote the electron from t₂g to e_g (a spin‑allowed, Laporte‑forbidden transition that gains intensity via vibronic coupling). The wavelength absorbed corresponds to the energy gap; the observed color is the complementary color of the absorbed light. For aqua ligands (weak field), Δ₀ lies in the visible region, typically resulting in absorption around yellow‑green, so the transmitted/reflected light appears violet. Slight distortions (Jahn–Teller effects for d¹ are small but possible) and solvation alter Δ₀ subtly, shifting hue. Charge‑transfer bands are generally weaker here than in π‑acceptor complexes; thus the color is dominated by the single d–d transition characteristic of an octahedral d¹ system. Temperature, concentration, and ligand substitution (e.g., replacing H₂O by stronger‑field ligands) will shift Δ₀ and the perceived color. This classic case illustrates how CFT relates electronic structure to color through the energy gap between split d levels.

Answer

d¹ (Ti³⁺): t₂g¹ → e_g¹ absorption ≈ Δ₀ (octahedral); absorbs yellow‑green → appears violet.
  • Weak‑field H₂O yields Δ₀ in visible range, enabling the d–d transition.
  • Vibronic coupling partially relaxes Laporte selection rule, giving observable intensity.

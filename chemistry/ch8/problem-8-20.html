d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.20

Original Question

Explain why transition metal ions are colored. Give examples of colored ions and their colors.

Background Concept — d-d Electronic Transitions and Color

The color of transition metal ions arises from d-d electronic transitions, where electrons absorb specific wavelengths of visible light and jump from lower energy d-orbitals to higher energy d-orbitals. In an isolated atom, all five d-orbitals have the same energy, but in a complex ion or crystal field, the d-orbitals split into different energy levels due to the electrostatic interaction with surrounding ligands. This splitting creates an energy gap (Δ) between the t₂g and eg orbitals in an octahedral field. When white light falls on the complex, electrons absorb photons with energy equal to this gap and undergo transitions from t₂g to eg orbitals. The absorbed wavelength corresponds to the complementary color of what we observe. For example, if a complex absorbs blue light (450-495 nm), it appears orange-red. The color intensity depends on the number of unpaired electrons and the nature of the ligand field. Strong field ligands cause greater splitting and shift absorption to shorter wavelengths, while weak field ligands cause smaller splitting and shift absorption to longer wavelengths. Ions with completely filled (d¹⁰) or empty (d⁰) d-orbitals are colorless because no d-d transitions are possible.

Answer

Transition metal ions are colored due to d-d electronic transitions:

  • Mechanism: Electrons absorb light and jump from t₂g to eg orbitals
  • Color observed: Complementary to the absorbed wavelength
  • Colorless ions: d⁰ (Ti⁴⁺) or d¹⁰ (Zn²⁺, Cd²⁺) configurations

Examples of colored ions:

Cu²⁺ (d⁹): Blue color
Fe³⁺ (d⁵): Yellow-brown color
Cr³⁺ (d³): Green color
Ni²⁺ (d⁸): Green color
Co²⁺ (d⁷): Pink color
Mn²⁺ (d⁵): Pale pink color

Tips to Solve

  • Only ions with partially filled d-orbitals show color.
  • d⁰ and d¹⁰ configurations are colorless (no d-d transitions possible).
  • Color depends on the energy gap between split d-orbitals.
  • Strong field ligands cause greater splitting → shorter wavelength absorption.
  • Weak field ligands cause smaller splitting → longer wavelength absorption.
  • Observed color is complementary to absorbed color.

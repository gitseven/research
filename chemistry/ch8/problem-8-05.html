d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.5

Original Question

What may be the stable oxidation state of the transition element with the following electron configurations in the ground state of their atoms: 3d¹, 3d², 3d³ and 3d⁹?

Background Concept — Oxidation States and d Count

Stable oxidation states relate to achieving favourable d‑electron counts (d⁰, d⁵, d¹⁰) upon ionisation and to lattice/solvation energies with common ligands. Early 3d metals (small d count) commonly show high oxidation states in oxides/fluorides; later ones stabilise lower states. Removing ns before (n−1)d electrons in cations means the neutral d count guides which cationic d count can be reached easily while maintaining CFSE/exchange benefits. For example, d¹ (like Sc) tends to +3 (d⁰), d² (Ti) stabilises +4 (d⁰) and +3 (d¹), d³ (V) stabilises +3 (d²) and +5 (d⁰ in oxides), d⁹ (Cu) stabilises +2 (d⁹ → JT active) and +1 (d¹⁰) in soft‑ligand environments.

Answer

  • 3d¹ → commonly +3 (d⁰) and +2 (d¹) less; +3 most stable in aqueous oxo chemistry.
  • 3d² → +4 (d⁰) and +3 (d¹) are common (Ti(IV), Ti(III)).
  • 3d³ → +3 (d²) and +5 (d⁰ in oxides/fluorides) possible (V(III), V(V)).
  • 3d⁹ → +2 (d⁹, Jahn–Teller) and +1 (d¹⁰) (Cu(II), Cu(I)).

Tips to Solve

  • Target d⁰, d⁵, d¹⁰ for enhanced stability depending on ligands.
  • Early 3d favour higher oxidation states in oxides/fluorides.
  • For d⁹, remember strong Jahn–Teller distortion stabilises +2.

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.9

Original Question

In what way is the electronic configuration of the transition elements different from the non‑transition elements?

Background Concept — Partially Filled d Subshells

Transition elements possess partially filled (n−1)d subshells in their atoms or in common oxidation states. Their valence shell thus involves both ns and (n−1)d electrons, enabling variable oxidation states and rich coordination chemistry. In contrast, representative (main‑group) elements typically have valence configurations involving only ns and np orbitals; their oxidation states often follow octet patterns and are more limited. The presence of low‑lying, closely spaced d orbitals in transition metals leads to crystal‑field and ligand‑field effects, d–d transitions (colour), and paramagnetism from unpaired d electrons. Additionally, electron removal in cations proceeds from ns before (n−1)d, a distinctive pattern not mirrored in main‑group elements. These configuration features underpin the metallic bonding strength (high atomisation enthalpies) and catalytic behaviour common in transition series.

Answer

  • Transition: valence includes (n−1)d and ns; partially filled d in atoms or common ions.
  • Non‑transition (main group): valence typically ns, np; no accessible d subshell of similar energy.
  • Consequences: variable oxidation states, colour, magnetism, catalysis vs more fixed valencies in main group.

Tips to Solve

  • Mention ns before (n−1)d removal in cations for transition metals.
  • Highlight d–d transitions and CFSE as d‑block signatures.
  • Contrast with octet‑driven ns/np configurations of main‑group elements.

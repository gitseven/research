d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.25

Original Question

Give examples and suggest reasons for the following features of transition metal chemistry: (i) The lowest oxide of transition metal is basic, the highest is amphoteric/acidic. (ii) A transition metal exhibits highest oxidation state in oxides and fluorides. (iii) The highest oxidation state is exhibited in oxoanions of a metal.

Background Concept — Oxidation States and Bonding

Oxidation states in transition-metal oxides and fluorides span wide ranges because metal–oxygen and metal–fluorine bonds can be highly covalent and stabilize high formal charges on the metal. At low oxidation states, metal oxides are more ionic, featuring basic O²⁻ and yielding basic character (e.g., MnO, FeO). As oxidation state increases, M–O bonds become more covalent and directional, the oxide lattice contains lower electron density on oxygen, and amphoteric to acidic behavior emerges (e.g., Mn₂O₇ is strongly acidic, CrO₃ forms chromic acid). Fluorine, being the most electronegative element, stabilizes high oxidation states via strong M–F bonds (e.g., PtF₆, OsF₆). Oxoanions such as permanganate (MnO₄⁻) and chromate (CrO₄²⁻) distribute negative charge over multiple electronegative oxygens through π and σ bonding, providing resonance stabilization to very high metal oxidation states (+7 in MnO₄⁻, +6 in CrO₄²⁻). These bonding patterns explain periodic trends in acidity/basicity of oxides and the propensity for high oxidation states in oxides/fluorides versus halides like chlorides or bromides.

Answer

  • (i) Basic → Amphoteric/Acidic with oxidation state: MnO, FeO (basic); Cr₂O₃ (amphoteric); CrO₃, Mn₂O₇ (acidic). Higher oxidation lowers electron density at oxygen and increases covalency, giving amphoteric/acidic behavior.
  • (ii) Highest oxidation in oxides/fluorides: OsO₄ (Os+8), Mn₂O₇ (Mn+7), CrO₃ (Cr+6); PtF₆ (Pt+6), OsF₆ (Os+6). Strong M–O/M–F bonds stabilize high positive charge.
  • (iii) Highest oxidation in oxoanions: MnO₄⁻ (Mn+7), CrO₄²⁻ (Cr+6), VO₄³⁻ (V+5). Resonance and charge delocalization over O atoms stabilize very high oxidation states.

Tips to Solve

  • Classify oxides by oxidation state: low (basic), intermediate (amphoteric), high (acidic).
  • Recall F and O stabilize high oxidation states via strong, electronegative bonds.
  • Oxoanions distribute charge; draw resonance forms to justify stability.

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.29

Original Question

The chemistry of actinoid elements is not so smooth as that of lanthanoids. Justify this statement by giving some examples from the oxidation state of these elements.

Background Concept — Variable Oxidation States in f-block

Lanthanoids generally show a predominant +3 oxidation state, with occasional +2 and +4 for specific elements (e.g., Eu²⁺, Yb²⁺, Ce⁴⁺). Their 4f orbitals are core-like, leading to limited participation in bonding and relatively uniform chemistry (“smooth” behavior). In contrast, actinoids have 5f orbitals that are more extended and overlap with ligand orbitals, enabling a wider range of accessible oxidation states (+3 to +7). As a result, actinoid chemistry is more complex and irregular across the series. For example, uranium exhibits +3, +4, +5, +6 (UO₂²⁺), neptunium and plutonium span +3 to +7, and americium commonly forms +3 and +4 states. This diversity results from the smaller energy gap between 5f, 6d, and 7s levels and greater covalency in actinoid–ligand bonding, making redox behavior and complex formation less predictable than lanthanoids.

Answer

  • Lanthanoids: Predominantly +3; limited +2 (Eu²⁺, Yb²⁺) and +4 (Ce⁴⁺, Tb⁴⁺).
  • Actinoids: Wide range: U (+3, +4, +5, +6), Np (+3 to +7), Pu (+3 to +7), Am (+3, +4), Cm (+3).
  • Reason: 5f orbitals participate in bonding (more covalent), small energy gaps among 5f/6d/7s → multiple oxidation states and irregular trends.

Tips to Solve

  • Contrast “+3 dominant” (lanthanoids) vs “multiple states” (actinoids).
  • Quote at least two actinoids with >3 oxidation states (U, Np, Pu).
  • Mention 5f orbital participation and covalency as the core reason.

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.19

Original Question

Explain the magnetic properties of transition elements. Why are some transition metal compounds paramagnetic while others are diamagnetic?

Background Concept — Magnetic Behavior in Transition Elements

Magnetic properties of transition elements arise from the presence of unpaired electrons in their d-orbitals. When electrons are unpaired, they have parallel spins and create a net magnetic moment, making the substance paramagnetic (attracted to magnetic fields). The magnetic moment (μ) is calculated using the formula: μ = √[n(n+2)] Bohr magnetons, where n is the number of unpaired electrons. Diamagnetic substances have all electrons paired, resulting in no net magnetic moment and weak repulsion from magnetic fields. The magnetic behavior depends on the electronic configuration and crystal field effects. In octahedral complexes, d-orbital splitting can cause pairing of electrons in the lower energy t₂g orbitals, reducing the number of unpaired electrons. High-spin complexes maintain maximum unpaired electrons and are more paramagnetic, while low-spin complexes have paired electrons and are less paramagnetic or diamagnetic. The strength of the ligand field determines whether high-spin or low-spin configuration is favored. Strong field ligands cause greater splitting and favor low-spin (paired) configuration, while weak field ligands cause less splitting and favor high-spin (unpaired) configuration.

Answer

Magnetic properties depend on unpaired electrons:

  • Paramagnetic: Contains unpaired electrons (e.g., Fe³⁺ with 5 unpaired d-electrons)
  • Diamagnetic: All electrons paired (e.g., Zn²⁺ with filled d¹⁰ configuration)
  • Magnetic moment: μ = √[n(n+2)] BM, where n = unpaired electrons

Examples:

Fe³⁺ (d⁵): 5 unpaired electrons → Strongly paramagnetic
Fe²⁺ (d⁶): 4 unpaired electrons (high-spin) or 0 unpaired (low-spin) → Paramagnetic or diamagnetic
Zn²⁺ (d¹⁰): 0 unpaired electrons → Diamagnetic

Tips to Solve

  • Count unpaired d-electrons in the electronic configuration.
  • Consider crystal field effects: high-spin vs low-spin complexes.
  • Strong field ligands (CN⁻, CO) favor low-spin (paired) configuration.
  • Weak field ligands (H₂O, F⁻) favor high-spin (unpaired) configuration.
  • Use μ = √[n(n+2)] to calculate magnetic moment.

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.14

Original Question

Describe the preparation of potassium dichromate from iron chromite ore. What is the effect of increasing pH on a solution of potassium dichromate?

Background Concept — Oxidative Alkaline Roasting and Chromate–Dichromate Equilibrium

Potassium dichromate is produced industrially from chromite (FeCr₂O₄) via oxidative alkaline roasting. The ore is blended with K₂CO₃ (or Na₂CO₃) and an oxidant (air/NaNO₃) and heated to form soluble chromate: 4FeCr₂O₄ + 8K₂CO₃ + 7O₂ → 8K₂CrO₄ + 2Fe₂O₃ + 8CO₂. The leach liquor containing K₂CrO₄ is separated from insoluble iron oxide. Acidification (H₂SO₄) converts chromate (yellow) to dichromate (orange): 2CrO₄²⁻ + 2H⁺ ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻ + H₂O. Potassium dichromate, being less soluble than the sodium salt, can be crystallised by adding K⁺ (KCl) or by using potassium carbonate initially. Purification follows by recrystallisation. The chromate–dichromate equilibrium is strongly pH‑dependent: increasing pH shifts the equilibrium to chromate (CrO₄²⁻, yellow), while decreasing pH favours dichromate (Cr₂O₇²⁻, orange). This interconversion mirrors the general acid–base behaviour of polyoxoanions (protonation leads to condensation). The oxidative power remains high in both forms, though speciation and redox potentials vary with acidity, impacting analytical and synthetic applications.

Answer

  • Roast FeCr₂O₄ with K₂CO₃ and air → leach K₂CrO₄ → acidify to Cr₂O₇²⁻ → crystallise K₂Cr₂O₇.
  • Effect of pH: higher pH shifts Cr₂O₇²⁻ ⇌ 2CrO₄²⁻ to the right (yellow chromate); lower pH favours dichromate (orange).

Tips to Solve

  • Remember: alkaline roasting → chromate; acidification → dichromate.
  • Quote the equilibrium: 2CrO₄²⁻ + 2H⁺ ⇌ Cr₂O₇²⁻ + H₂O; pH controls direction.
  • Note colour change: yellow (chromate) ↔ orange (dichromate).

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.23

Original Question

Which metal in the first series of transition metals exhibits +1 oxidation state most frequently and why?

Background Concept — Stability of Low Oxidation States

Across the first transition series (Sc to Zn), common oxidation states arise from the involvement of (n−1)d and ns electrons. The +1 state corresponds to loss of a single ns electron without engaging the d-electrons. Such a low oxidation state is favored when removal of only the ns electron gives an especially stable electronic configuration, often a half‑filled or filled d-subshell, and when lattice or solvation energies are sufficient to stabilize the resulting cation. Copper is notable because Cu (3d¹⁰ 4s¹) readily forms Cu⁺ by losing the 4s electron to reach a filled 3d¹⁰ configuration, which is comparatively stable. Silver and gold show similar behavior in their respective series. By contrast, early transition metals have higher energies for the +1 state due to unstable d-configurations and a preference for higher oxidation states driven by strong metal–ligand bonding and high lattice energies. In aqueous solution, Cu⁺ is prone to disproportionation (2Cu⁺ → Cu²⁺ + Cu), yet in solids (e.g., Cu₂O) and in soft‑donor ligand environments, the +1 state is stabilized by covalency and lattice enthalpy.

Answer

Copper exhibits the +1 oxidation state most frequently in the first transition series because losing the 4s electron yields a filled 3d¹⁰ shell, which is relatively stable. The +1 state is further stabilized in solids (e.g., Cu₂O) and with soft ligands.

Cu (3d¹⁰ 4s¹) → Cu⁺ (3d¹⁰) + e⁻
2Cu⁺(aq) ⇌ Cu²⁺(aq) + Cu(s) (disproportionation in water)

Tips to Solve

  • Check post‑ionization d-electron count; filled (d¹⁰) shells are especially stable.
  • Consider phase: Cu⁺ is unstable in water but stable in solids like Cu₂O.
  • Soft ligands (PPh₃, CN⁻) stabilize lower oxidation states via covalency.

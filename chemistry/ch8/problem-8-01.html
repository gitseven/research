d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.1

Original Question

Write the electronic configuration of the following ions: (i) P (V) (ii) Co (III) (iii) Mn (VIII) (iv) Th.

Background Concept — Building Configurations for Ions

Electronic configurations are assigned by filling orbitals according to the aufbau principle (increasing n + l, then n), Hund’s rule (maximum multiplicity with parallel spins), and the Pauli exclusion principle. For ions, electrons are removed first from the highest principal quantum number shell (valence ns) and then from (n−1)d for transition elements. Thus, for first‑row transition metals, a common pitfall is removing 4s before 3d when forming cations. For main‑group p‑block elements such as phosphorus, the oxidation state V (+5) indicates removal of five valence electrons, resulting in a noble‑gas‑like core. For very high oxidation states like Mn(VIII), all 3d and 4s electrons are removed, yielding a 3d⁰ species; such states are typically stabilised in oxoanions/oxides (e.g., MnO₄⁻), not as free ions in solution. Actinoids such as thorium (Th) involve 5f/6d/7s shells; their ground‑state configurations reflect close energetic spacing and relativistic effects, but a commonly accepted configuration for Th is [Rn] 6d² 7s². When writing concise configurations, use noble‑gas cores (e.g., [Ar], [Kr], [Xe], [Rn]) to emphasise valence shells responsible for chemistry. For accuracy, remember small anomalies (Cr, Cu) in neutrals and apply the ionisation rule (remove ns before (n−1)d) when forming cations. These guidelines ensure correct electron counts and d‑electron tallies crucial for magnetism, color, and crystal field arguments used throughout transition metal chemistry.

Answer

  • (i) P(V): P⁵⁺ has configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ = [Ne].
  • (ii) Co(III): Co atom [Ar] 3d⁷ 4s²; Co³⁺ → remove 4s² then one 3d → [Ar] 3d⁶.
  • (iii) Mn(VIII): Mn atom [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s²; Mn⁸⁺ → remove 7 valence electrons (2 from 4s, 5 from 3d) → [Ar] 3d⁰ = [Ar].
  • (iv) Th: ≈ [Rn] 6d² 7s² (often written [Rn] 6d² 7s²; minor 5f/6d mixing may be cited in advanced texts).

Tips to Solve

  • For transition metal cations, remove ns electrons before (n−1)d electrons.
  • Use noble‑gas shorthand; double‑check total electron count matches the ionic charge.
  • High oxidation states (+7, +8) often imply d⁰ configurations in oxides/oxoanions.

d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.10

Original Question

What are the different oxidation states exhibited by the lanthanoids?

Background Concept — Predominant +3 with Occasional +2 and +4

Lanthanoids (La–Lu) characteristically exhibit a dominant +3 oxidation state, arising from loss of the two 6s electrons and one 5d/4f electron, giving Ln³⁺ with contracted 4f orbitals that are core‑like and weakly involved in bonding. Nevertheless, several lanthanoids access +2 and +4 states when stabilised by lattice or complexation energies and favourable 4f electron counts. The +2 state is common for Eu and Yb (f⁷ and f¹⁴, respectively) and occurs for Sm, Tm, Dy under appropriate conditions. The +4 state is most accessible for Ce and Tb (and sometimes Pr), where achieving f⁰ (Ce⁴⁺) or half‑filled subshells confers stability; Pr⁴⁺ can be stabilised in oxides/fluorides. The relative inertness of 4f orbitals means ligand field effects are weaker than for d metals, so trends are dominated by lattice energies, solvation, and the special stability of f⁰, f⁷, and f¹⁴ configurations. These provide the key rationale for which ions display +2 or +4 in addition to +3.

Answer

  • Predominant: +3 for all lanthanoids.
  • +2: especially Eu²⁺, Yb²⁺ (also Sm²⁺, Tm²⁺, Dy²⁺ in some solids/solutions).
  • +4: notably Ce⁴⁺ (f⁰), Tb⁴⁺; Pr⁴⁺ in certain compounds.

Tips to Solve

  • Anchor on +3 as the default; add +2 (Eu, Yb) and +4 (Ce, Tb) as exceptions.
  • Justify with favoured f counts: f⁰, f⁷, f¹⁴.
  • Mention weak ligand‑field influence on 4f; lattice/solvation dominate.

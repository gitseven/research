d- and f-Block Elements · Problem 8.4

Original Question

To what extent do the electronic configurations decide the stability of oxidation states in the first series of the transition elements? Illustrate with examples.

Background Concept — d-electron Count, CFSE, Exchange, Lattice

Stability of oxidation states in 3d metals reflects a competition among gas‑phase ionisation energies, crystal‑field stabilisation energy (CFSE), exchange energy, and lattice/solvation energies of the resulting ions/complexes. Electron configuration informs CFSE and exchange: half‑filled (d⁵) and fully filled (d¹⁰) subshells are particularly stable by exchange and pairing considerations; specific fields (octahedral, tetrahedral, square planar) yield CFSE that depends on d electron count and spin state. For example, Mn²⁺ (d⁵, high spin) is unusually stable in water; oxidation to Mn³⁺ (d⁴) loses exchange stabilisation and is disfavoured unless strong‑field ligands lower the energy. Conversely, Fe³⁺ (d⁵) is favoured relative to Fe²⁺ (d⁶) by achieving the half‑filled configuration. High oxidation states are stabilised by strongly electronegative ligands (O²⁻, F⁻) that provide large lattice energies and strong σ/π bonding: e.g., Cr(VI) in chromates/dichromates and Mn(VII) in permanganate arise from strong M=O multiple bonding and anionic lattice stabilisation. In low‑coordination, strong‑field environments, d⁸ ions (Ni²⁺, Pd²⁺, Pt²⁺) adopt square‑planar geometry, gaining CFSE and stabilising +2. Thus, while configurations do not solely determine stability, they strongly bias which oxidation states dominate under given ligands and media via CFSE/exchange patterns coupled to lattice/solvation thermodynamics.

Answer

  • Mn²⁺(d⁵) and Fe³⁺(d⁵) stabilised by half‑filled shells; Fe³⁺ favoured over Fe²⁺ in acid.
  • Cr(VI) in CrO₄²⁻/Cr₂O₇²⁻ and Mn(VII) in MnO₄⁻ stabilised by M=O bonding and lattice energies.
  • d⁸ ions (Ni²⁺/Pd²⁺/Pt²⁺) stabilise square‑planar complexes, favouring +2.

Tips to Solve

  • Map d counts to CFSE (high‑spin vs low‑spin) for the geometry in question.
  • Invoke lattice/solvation when comparing high oxidation states in oxides/fluorides.
  • Use half‑filled/full‑filled arguments (d⁵, d¹⁰) to rationalise trends.

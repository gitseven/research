Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.5

Original Question

A first order reaction has a rate constant 1.15 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. How long will 5 g of this reactant take to reduce to 3 g?

Background Concept — First-Order Kinetics and Integrated Law

For first-order processes, the rate of decay is proportional to the current amount of reactant, leading to an exponential decay law: dN/dt = −kN. Integrating with respect to time gives N(t) = N₀ e^(−kt), or equivalently ln(N₀/N) = kt. Because concentration and mass are proportional for a single species in a fixed volume, ratios N₀/N are directly replaced by mass ratios m₀/m when volume and density are constant. The time to reach a given fraction f of the initial amount is t = (1/k) ln(1/f). The half-life t₁/₂ = (ln 2)/k is independent of initial amount, a diagnostic hallmark of first-order behaviour. In practical problems, ensure consistent units for k (e.g., s⁻¹) and t (s). If k is given in s⁻¹ and times are desired in minutes or hours, convert after solving. This framework is ubiquitous in chemical kinetics (unimolecular reactions), nuclear decay, pharmacokinetics (first-order elimination), and atmospheric lifetimes of trace species. When data are noisy, plotting ln(amount) vs time yields a straight line of slope −k, facilitating extraction of k by linear regression.

Answer

t = (1/k) ln(m₀/m) = (1/1.15×10⁻³) ln(5/3) ≈ 869.6 × 0.5108 ≈ 444 s (≈ 7.4 min)

Tips to Solve

  • Use the integrated first-order law with a ratio; absolute units cancel out.
  • Match time units to k; convert seconds to minutes at the end if needed.
  • Check that the result is sensible: reducing to 60% should take less than one half-life.

Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.16

Original Question

The rate constant for a first order reaction is 60 s⁻¹. How much time will it take to reduce the initial concentration of the reactant to its 1/16th value?

Background Concept — Successive Half-lives

First-order decay implies that each half-life reduces the amount by a factor of 2, regardless of the starting concentration. Reaching 1/16 of the initial amount corresponds to passing through four half-lives (since 2⁴ = 16). The half-life is t₁/₂ = (ln 2)/k, so the required time is t = 4 t₁/₂ = 4(ln 2)/k. This method avoids computing logarithms directly, though one can equivalently use t = (1/k) ln(1/f) with f = 1/16 to obtain the same result (t = (1/k) ln 16). The constancy of the half-life is unique to first-order processes; for other orders, the number of half-lives to reach a given fraction depends on [A]₀.

Answer

t = (1/k) ln 16 = (1/60) × 2.7726 = 4.62×10⁻² s

Equivalently, 4 t₁/₂ with t₁/₂ = 0.693/60 = 0.01155 s → 4 × 0.01155 = 0.0462 s.

Tips to Solve

  • Use half-life counting for powers of two; use ln for arbitrary fractions.
  • Check units: k in s⁻¹ gives time in seconds.
  • Sanity check: four half-lives at ~0.0116 s each ≈ 0.046 s total.

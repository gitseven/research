Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.9

Original Question

The activation energy for the reaction 2HI(g) → H₂(g) + I₂(g) is 209.5 kJ mol⁻¹ at 581 K. Calculate the fraction of molecules of reactants having energy equal to or greater than activation energy.

Background Concept — Energy Distribution and Arrhenius Factor

In gas‑phase kinetics, not all molecules carry the same kinetic (or internal) energy at a given temperature; instead, energies are distributed according to the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution. Reaction events require that colliding molecules possess sufficient energy to overcome the activation barrier Eₐ (and appropriate orientation). The Arrhenius expression k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT) embeds the fraction of energetically capable molecules within the exponential term e^(−Eₐ/RT), often referred to as the Boltzmann factor. While the true fraction depends on the full distribution (and on orientation/entropy terms), a common approximation for examination problems is to estimate the fraction of molecules with energy ≥ Eₐ as f ≈ e^(−Eₐ/RT). This approach captures the dominant exponential sensitivity of the reactive fraction to Eₐ and T: higher temperatures dramatically increase f, whereas larger Eₐ reduces it exponentially. The pre‑exponential factor A accounts for collision frequency and steric requirements; however, for the specific question of energetic eligibility, e^(−Eₐ/RT) suffices. Careful attention to units is essential: use Eₐ in J mol⁻¹, R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹, and T in kelvin. The resulting fraction can be exceedingly small for large barriers at modest temperatures, highlighting why many gas‑phase reactions are slow without heating or catalysis.

Answer

Eₐ = 209.5 kJ mol⁻¹ = 2.095×10⁵ J mol⁻¹; R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹; T = 581 K
f ≈ e^(−Eₐ/RT) = exp(−2.095×10⁵ / (8.314×581)) = exp(−43.4) ≈ 1.5×10⁻¹9

Tips to Solve

  • Convert Eₐ to J mol⁻¹ before substitution; keep T in kelvin.
  • Use the exponential key carefully; very small values are expected for large barriers.
  • Remember this is an approximation for the energetic fraction, ignoring orientation factors.

Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.13

Original Question

Calculate the half-life of a first order reaction from their rate constants given below: (i) 200 s⁻¹ (ii) 2 min⁻¹ (iii) 4 years⁻¹

Background Concept — Universal First-order Half-life

For first-order kinetics, the decay law [A](t) = [A]₀ e^(−kt) implies that the time required to reduce the amount to any fixed fraction is independent of the initial concentration. Setting [A](t₁/₂) = [A]₀/2 yields t₁/₂ = (ln 2)/k ≈ 0.693/k. This simple reciprocal relationship allows rapid interconversion between k and half-life across chemistry, physics, and biology. Because k carries units of reciprocal time, t₁/₂ naturally inherits the time unit reciprocal to k’s (e.g., if k is in s⁻¹, t₁/₂ is in seconds). The logarithmic nature of exponential decay means that equal decrements in the natural log of concentration occur in equal time steps. This trait is exploited in linearised plots (ln[A] vs t) where the slope is −k and the intercept is ln[A]₀. In multistep mechanisms, apparent first-order behaviour can arise under pseudo-first-order conditions when one reactant is in large excess; in such cases, the observed half-life pertains to the effective rate constant at those conditions. Care with units and significant figures is essential when k is reported in unconventional time bases (minutes⁻¹, years⁻¹). Converting t₁/₂ between units after calculation ensures clarity for comparison.

Answer

(i) t₁/₂ = 0.693/200 = 3.465×10⁻³ s
(ii) t₁/₂ = 0.693/2 = 0.3465 min ≈ 20.79 s
(iii) t₁/₂ = 0.693/4 = 0.17325 years

Tips to Solve

  • Use t₁/₂ = 0.693/k directly; keep k and t in reciprocal units.
  • Convert to desired units after calculating (e.g., minutes to seconds).
  • Check magnitudes: larger k → shorter t₁/₂.

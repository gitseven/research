Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.8

Original Question

The rate of the chemical reaction doubles for an increase of 10 K in absolute temperature from 298 K. Calculate Eₐ.

Background Concept — Two-Point Arrhenius

When rate constants at two temperatures are known (or related by a ratio), the Arrhenius equation can be written in ratio form without needing A explicitly: ln(k₂/k₁) = −Eₐ/R (1/T₂ − 1/T₁). This expression isolates Eₐ given the temperature change and the observed change in k. Small temperature intervals around room temperature often produce factors of ~2 per 10 K for moderate activation energies; the exact Eₐ is determined by substituting the measured ratio. Using base‑10 logs is equally valid if consistently converted: log(k₂/k₁) = (−Eₐ/2.303R)(1/T₂ − 1/T₁). Careful handling of sign and the (1/T) difference is crucial; note that if T₂ > T₁ and k₂ > k₁, then 1/T₂ − 1/T₁ < 0, making the right-hand side positive as required by ln(k₂/k₁) > 0.

Answer

Given k₂/k₁ = 2, T₁ = 298 K, T₂ = 308 K
Eₐ = R ln 2 / (1/T₁ − 1/T₂) = 8.314×0.693 / (1/298 − 1/308) ≈ 5.29×10⁴ J mol⁻¹ ≈ 52.9 kJ mol⁻¹

Tips to Solve

  • Use the ratio form directly to avoid computing A.
  • Compute (1/T₁ − 1/T₂) carefully; it is positive when T₂ > T₁ in the denominator form used above.
  • Keep units consistent; R in J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ yields Eₐ in J mol⁻¹.

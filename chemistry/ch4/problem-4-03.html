Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.3

Original Question

For a reaction, A + B → Product; the rate law is r = k[A]^n[B]^m. Write the order of the reaction.

Background Concept — Molecularity vs Order, Overall Order

The order of a reaction is an empirical quantity derived from the rate law, indicating how the instantaneous rate depends on reactant concentrations. For a general rate law r = k[A]^n[B]^m..., the overall order is the sum of exponents n + m + ..., while the partial orders are n with respect to A and m with respect to B. These exponents need not be integers and must be determined experimentally (e.g., via initial rates or isolation method), as they reflect the mechanism’s rate-determining step and may differ from stoichiometric coefficients. This contrasts with molecularity, a theoretical count of molecules involved in a single elementary event, which is inherently an integer and not directly measurable for complex reactions. Knowledge of orders guides integrated rate law selection (first order: ln[A] vs t linear; second order: 1/[A] vs t; zero order: [A] vs t) and informs half-life behavior and sensitivity to concentration changes. When catalysts, inhibitors, or complex formation are present, apparent orders can shift with conditions. Thus, when a rate law is provided, computing the overall order is straightforward: sum the concentration exponents. This quantity provides a compact descriptor of kinetic dependence and is essential for comparing and categorising reaction kinetics across systems.

Answer

Order = n + m

Tips to Solve

  • Sum the exponents of all concentration terms present in the rate law.
  • Do not use stoichiometric coefficients unless the reaction is elementary and stated as such.
  • Remember that orders may be fractional or zero; report the sum as given.

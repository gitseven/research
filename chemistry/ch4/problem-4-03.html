Chapter 4 · Problem 3

Question

For a first-order reaction A → B, if the initial concentration of A is 0.1 M and after 100 seconds it becomes 0.05 M, calculate the rate constant.

Background Concept — Integrated Rate Law

For a first-order reaction A → B, the integrated rate law is ln([A]₀/[A]ₜ) = kt, where [A]₀ is initial concentration, [A]ₜ is concentration at time t, k is rate constant, and t is time. The half-life of a first-order reaction is t₁/₂ = 0.693/k and is independent of initial concentration.

Visualization

Solution

Using the integrated rate law for first-order reaction: ln([A]₀/[A]ₜ) = kt. Given [A]₀ = 0.1 M, [A]ₜ = 0.05 M, t = 100 s. ln(0.1/0.05) = k × 100. ln(2) = 100k. 0.693 = 100k. Therefore k = 0.693/100 = 0.00693 s⁻¹.

Tips to Solve

  • Use ln([A]₀/[A]ₜ) = kt for first-order
  • Calculate the ratio [A]₀/[A]ₜ
  • Take natural logarithm
  • Solve for k
  • Check units (s⁻¹ for first-order)

Key Formulas

First-order: ln([A]₀/[A]ₜ) = kt; Half-life: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k

Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.10

Original Question

What is the order of the reaction with respect to A and B?

Background Concept — Determining Partial Orders

The order of a reaction with respect to a given reactant is the exponent of its concentration in the rate law. For a rate law of the form r = k[A]^n[B]^m, the partial orders are n (with respect to A) and m (with respect to B). These must be obtained experimentally because they reflect the mechanism’s kinetics rather than the overall stoichiometric equation. Two common experimental strategies are used. (1) Initial rates method: holding [B] constant while varying [A] allows one to measure how the initial rate scales with [A]; from v ∝ [A]^n, a log–log plot log v vs log [A] yields a slope equal to n. Repeating with [A] constant while varying [B] yields m. (2) Isolation method: keeping one reactant in large excess makes its concentration effectively constant, so the rate law reduces to pseudo‑order in the other reactant; time–course data then fit an integrated law (e.g., first‑order if n = 1) to determine the order. Additional checks include half‑life dependence (first order: t₁/₂ independent of [A]₀; second order: t₁/₂ ∝ 1/[A]₀) and linearity of integrated plots (e.g., ln[A] vs t). Once n and m are known, the overall order is n + m. Accurate determination requires temperature control (k is T‑dependent), constant ionic strength for ionic reactions, and avoiding side reactions or product inhibition during the initial rate window.

Answer

With respect to A: the exponent of [A] in the rate law (denote it n). With respect to B: the exponent of [B] (denote it m). Determine n and m from initial‑rates or isolation experiments.

Tips to Solve

  • Use log v vs log [A] (B constant) to get slope = n; swap roles to get m.
  • If one reactant is in large excess, fit a pseudo‑first/second‑order integrated plot to extract the order.
  • Check half‑life behavior as a diagnostic of first vs second order.

Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.14

Original Question

The half-life for radioactive decay of ¹⁴C is 5730 years. An archaeological artifact containing wood had only 80% of the ¹⁴C found in a living tree. Estimate the age of the sample.

Background Concept — First-order Decay and Dating

Radioactive decay follows first-order kinetics: N(t) = N₀ e^(−λt), where λ is the decay constant. The half-life t₁/₂ and λ are related by t₁/₂ = ln 2 / λ, so λ = 0.693/t₁/₂. Radiocarbon dating exploits the fact that living organisms maintain a roughly constant ratio of ¹⁴C/¹²C by exchanging CO₂ with the atmosphere. After death, exchange stops and ¹⁴C decays, reducing the ¹⁴C level relative to living material. Measuring the remaining fraction f = N/N₀ yields age via t = (1/λ) ln(1/f). Because the half-life is long, small analytical errors in f can lead to substantial uncertainty in t, so high-precision measurements and corrections for isotopic fractionation and past atmospheric ¹⁴C variations are used in advanced protocols (e.g., calibration curves). For textbook problems, treating decay as ideal first‑order with the tabulated half-life suffices to estimate ages to first approximation.

Answer

λ = 0.693 / 5730 y = 1.209×10⁻⁴ y⁻¹
f = 0.80 → t = (1/λ) ln(1/0.80) = (5730/0.693) × 0.2231 ≈ 1843 years

Tips to Solve

  • Use t = (1/λ) ln(N₀/N) with λ = 0.693/t₁/₂.
  • Express the remaining activity or ¹⁴C as a fraction f of the modern level.
  • Check order of magnitude: 80% remaining is well under one half-life (~2k years).

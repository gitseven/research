Chemical Kinetics · Problem 4.22

Original Question

The rate constant for the decomposition of N₂O₅ at various temperatures is given below: Draw a graph between ln k and 1/T and calculate the values of A and Eₐ. Predict the rate constant at 30° and 50°C.

Background Concept — Linearising the Arrhenius Equation

The Arrhenius equation, k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT), describes how rate constants vary with temperature. Taking natural logs yields ln k = ln A − Eₐ/(RT), a straight‑line relationship in variables y = ln k and x = 1/T with slope m = −Eₐ/R and intercept b = ln A. When experimental k values are provided at several temperatures, one performs a linear regression of ln k against 1/T to obtain m and b; then Eₐ = −mR and A = e^b. Using multiple points (rather than two) averages experimental error and provides confidence intervals for Eₐ and A. Units must be consistent: Eₐ in J mol⁻¹ when R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹; T in kelvin; k in s⁻¹ (for first order). Once Eₐ and A are known, prediction at a new temperature T′ follows immediately from k(T′) = A e^(−Eₐ/(RT′)). As a practical check, plotting the data ensures linearity over the studied temperature window; systematic curvature suggests a change in mechanism, diffusion control, or temperature‑dependent pre‑exponential factors. For classroom problems, data typically adhere well to a straight line and yield physically reasonable A (often 10¹²–10¹⁴ s⁻¹ for unimolecular gas reactions) and Eₐ (tens to hundreds of kJ mol⁻¹).

Answer

From the line ln k = b + mx: Eₐ = −mR, A = e^b. Then k(303 K) and k(323 K) are computed via k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT). Insert numerical m, b from your regression.

Tips to Solve

  • Convert °C to K (add 273.15) before inverting T.
  • Use linear regression over all points; avoid relying on a single pair.
  • Check that predicted k at higher T is larger than at lower T.
